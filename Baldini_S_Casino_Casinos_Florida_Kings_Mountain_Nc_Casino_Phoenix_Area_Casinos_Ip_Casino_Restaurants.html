Inpay: a fast-growing payment method with big plans

There's an old English expression that says 'necessity is the mother of invention'. This is certainly the case when it comes to Inpay and its founder Jacob Tackmann Thomsen.

After the worst cyclone in Myanmar's history devastated the country in 2008, Tackmann Thomsen felt compelled to help. He went online to make a donation to the thousands of orphaned children there, but was shocked to discover that his payment would lose 5% to fees and take several days to arrive.

Determined to get all of the money to where it was needed, he set about finding a way to make cross-border payments cheap and quick, and Inpay was the product of his search.

Starting out as an office in Copenhagen, the start-up quickly grew into one of Denmark's top payment methods, and the firm opened a second office in London as it looked to expand internationally. Tackmann Thomsen had timed it just right: his idea allowed him to tap into the online payment industry that was about to start booming.

In 2018, Inpay acquired Eurogiro, a global payment community for postal operators that broadened the start-up's service offerings. The Financial Times named it one of the fastest-growing companies in Europe in 2021 after it processed several millions of transactions that year.

Along with Eurogiro, Inpay is targeting a customer base of 100 million people by 2030 — a figure that would put it among the most successful payment methods in the world.

The pros and cons of Inpay

Inpay looks the part with its smooth, modern website and rapidly growing customer base, but how does its service measure up?

Well, the most important aspect of any bank transfer is security. We want to be 100% sure that our money will arrive at its intended destination when we want it to. Inpay scores highly on this front. It follows stringent fraud detection measures, is regulated by the Danish Financial Supervisory Authority (FSA), and is an EU-recognized institution. There are also no user complaints online about Inpay's service, which suggests that it is held in high regard by its customers.

Inpay also places great emphasis on transaction speeds. Its website claims a typical time of just minutes, which beats traditional bank transfers, but it is about normal for modern digital methods. Still, you can expect a quick transfer once you've completed the payment details.

In terms of cost, the only information we could find on the site was that it costs less than a wire transfer, which isn't saying much. That said, payment methods like this are often cheap, if not free, for users, because they use a single integration method. This involves having partner accounts in each country rather than transferring the funds and then converting them into the local currency.

Inpay has a large network of participating banks, but if your bank isn't part of it, then you won't be able to use the service. This is an obvious downside compared to more widely available methods like PayPal. It's also unavailable in the United States, one of the largest consumer markets in the world.
