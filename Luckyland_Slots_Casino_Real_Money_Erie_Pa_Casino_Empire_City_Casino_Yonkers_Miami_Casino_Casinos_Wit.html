India's land-based gambling laws

Gambling laws in India are complex and vary from state to state. Despite there being a national Gambling Act (1867) that prohibits running (and visiting) a public gambling house, the Indian Constitution, formed in 1949, gives states the power to create their own gambling laws in its Seventh Schedule (34, List II), which the government cannot interfere in.

To date, only three states allow land-based casinos: Goa, Daman and Sikkim, however, even these are subject to restrictions. The states released a combined Public Gambling Act in 1976 which dictates that only five-star hotels and offshore vessels can house a casino, and only with the regional government's permission. It means there are still relatively few casinos to choose from, with tourist-friendly Goa hosting ten — including a few 'floating casinos' — and Sikkim and Daman only a handful between them.

The law varies across the country's other 25 states. Nagaland and Maharashtra are two states that have passed acts banning gambling, while others discourage the practice but have certain grey areas. These are too numerous to mention in this article, so, like with all law-related issues, it's always a good idea for players to read up on their local state laws before taking part in any gambling activity.

Note: Like in many countries that have strict anti-gambling laws, a betting black market has thrived for many years in sports betting and illegal casinos. Its obvious lack of regulation means that much of the money finds its way in the hands of criminals, which has led to many people calling for the national legalization of gambling in India. Whether this will happen is doubtful, but the argument for a regulated system is a strong one.

Online gambling laws in India

Online gambling is a relatively recent phenomenon in India, so most national and state laws don't mention it as an activity. Even the national Information Technology Act of 2000, designed to regulate cyber activities, does not mention virtual gambling, and therefore is open to interpretation.

That said, many states do actively outlaw the practice, and anyone caught breaking the law may face severe punishment. For that reason, like with land-based betting, it's recommendable to always check the local law regarding online gambling before playing.

Despite this, some states have tried to liberalize their online gambling laws. They have a legal online lottery and tried (but failed) to offer several online gambling licences in 2010. This suggests that there could be an online gambling market for domestic operators in certain places in the future.

While gambling on domestic sites is pretty much forbidden across the country, it's important to stress that visiting offshore, or non-Indian, sites is legal for Indian players. Indian law does not specifically ban those, meaning players won't face punishment, although this could change in the future.
