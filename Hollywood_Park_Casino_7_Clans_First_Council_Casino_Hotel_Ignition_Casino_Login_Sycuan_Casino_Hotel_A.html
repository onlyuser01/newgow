Giropay: Germany's leading online payment method

Deutsche Bank is the biggest bank in Germany, and it was from their retail arm, Postbank, that Giropay was founded in February 2006. Their purpose was simple: to provide a secure payment service to serve the growing online market.

It was a smart move. By May of the following year, Giropay users had put more than 100 million euros through the service. This had nearly doubled to €185 million the year after, and today Giropay processes over a million transfers a month.

The main reason for their success is their commitment to real-time instant transfers. Users can be confident that their money reaches the merchant straight away, which allows for the quick shipment of their item, or activation of a service.

Transfers are also extremely secure — they have to go through the user's bank to get permission: this often takes the form of two-factor authentication (2FA), or PIN-based access, and means that no bank details are shared with the merchant. Giropay also follows strict data protection rules and vows that all data transfers are SSL encrypted.

Giropay is recognized as a leading internet payment method today, reaching millions of German and Austrian users and serving the majority of the online market in the two countries.

Pros and cons of Giropay

Giropay users benefit from the tight security features mentioned previously. By simply acting as a conduit between the bank and merchant, they let the user's bank deal with the primary security process, and employ SSL encryption and data protection once the transfer takes place.

Their service is also free of charge for the customer. Giropay make their money from charging the merchant fees, so you won't have to pay a cent — the only fees you'll need to consider are those imposed by your bank, so it's worth checking these out before making the transfer.

You can also count on your payment arriving immediately — a great advantage if you're looking to play casino games right away. Giropay also offer customer support if the recipient claims not to have received the money: their online statement is legal proof of the transfer, and they offer help to the customer in the event of a dispute.

If you're based outside Germany and Austria, however, then Giropay isn't available to you unfortunately. This is a shame, as many more users could benefit from their quality service, but there are providers in other countries who offer similar methods.

Another slight negative is that payments are only guaranteed up to a certain amount. While this is subject to change in the future, it does mean that transferring a large amount via Giropay carries an element of risk: in fact, Giropay don't offer buyer protection, so if something does go wrong, you won't be covered.
