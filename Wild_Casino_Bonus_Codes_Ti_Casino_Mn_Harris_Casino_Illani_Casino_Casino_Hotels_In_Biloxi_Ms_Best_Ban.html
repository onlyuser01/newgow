Bancontact: one of Belgium's premier payment methods

While the current company has only been in existence since 2018, Bancontact can trace its origin back to 1979 when it was launched as a payment scheme by a trio of Belgian banks.

In 1989, it merged with rival system Mister Cash to form Banksys, a successful electronic payment method. When Atos Wordline acquired Banksys in 2006, Belgian shareholders were allowed to continue the Bancontact name as a new payment scheme within the country. This provided the launchpad for the company that exists today.

In May 2014, Bancontact launched a new payment app that let users pay for supermarket products by scanning a QR code and entering a PIN. They could also pay for goods online and transfer funds to their friends. The scheme exploded in popularity, to the point that, together with rival firm Payconiq, it was responsible for 15 million transactions in 2017.

The following year, the two dominant players announced their merger to form Bancontact Payconiq. The focus ever since has been on the growing contactless payment market, helped by hygiene-conscious public mentality brought about by the COVID-19 pandemic.

Several banks have signed up to the Bancontact payment scheme, opening up a much wider market to the company. Users can now use the method to pay for public transport tickets, buy products online, order a takeaway or settle their energy bills. The contactless feature was pushed further with the launch of 'wearables': smart rings, bracelets, watches and key rings that facilitate digital payments through the user holding them next to a payment terminal.

Today, Bancontact continues to dominate the Belgian e-payments market: it's available on Apple Store and Google Play and is available to use with thousands of stores and websites across Europe.
