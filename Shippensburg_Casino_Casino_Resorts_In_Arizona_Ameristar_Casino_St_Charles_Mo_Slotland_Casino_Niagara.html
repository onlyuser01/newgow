ecoVoucher – a convenient way of paying for online goods in cash

ecoVoucher first hit the market in February 2015 as an alternative to Ecocard's (now known as ecoPayz) prepaid card and digital wallet. Like ecoPayz, ecoVoucher is a trading name of PSI-Pay Ltd, a UK-based company regulated by the Financial Services Authority (FSA).

The concept of ecoVoucher appealed to a demographic of online users who preferred the security of paying in cash. They didn't need to share any bank details or open a new account: they just went to an ecoVoucher-affiliated store, paid the required amount in cash, and received a PIN in return. They then entered this number into the website they wanted to purchase from when they got home.

The pros and cons of ecoVoucher

Security is at the heart of ecoVoucher's service, and we'd call this the biggest advantage of using it. You don't have to share any banking info or personal information at any point: you simply pay for the PIN and enter it online. The one flaw to this plan is that you could lose the PIN, of course. If this happens, then it would effectively be the same as losing the cash equivalent, unless the store kindly issues you with another voucher.

ecoVoucher is extremely simple to use. All of us have bought stuff in a bricks-and-mortar store before, and entering 18 digits into a computer is hardly rocket science.

Another nice advantage of ecoVoucher is that it's free. The service makes its money through charging vendors commission for each sale.

On the negative side, ecoVouchers are only available in amounts up to $/€/£150, and users can use one voucher per transaction. For purchases higher than this number, ecoVoucher recommends using ecoPayz, which can handle higher amounts. This limit is pretty restrictive: surely this method is better for larger transactions as the user has personal control over his or her money?

It's also important to remember that ecoVoucher is only available in the three currencies mentioned: the US dollar, the Euro, and the British Pound. While these are three major tenders, they might not appeal to a lot of people, particularly in Latin America, Asia, and Africa, who prefer to use local money when paying in cash.
