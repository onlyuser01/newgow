Land-based gambling in New Zealand

Gambling is a popular pastime in New Zealand and has probably existed since the first European settlement there in the late 18th century.

The introduction of the 1908 Gambling Act placed heavy restrictions on the practice, however, with legal gambling limited to racetracks – a policy in line with the more puritanical thinking of the time.

Despite these measures, gambling continued to be a common activity and 1951 saw the country relax its laws to allow lotteries – the famous Golden Kiwi draw being the first – slot machines, and a few casinos dotted around the nation.

Today, much of the gambling action takes place via 'pokies' — the word New Zealanders use for slot machines. These arrived in the country in the late 1980s and are owned by charitable organizations rather than commercial enterprises.

Note: While gamblers still have to be careful to gamble safely, they can play with the knowledge that a percentage of their stake goes to charity and public causes, such as the building of a $402 million convention center in Auckland.

Despite fairly relaxed gambling laws, there are still only a handful of legal casinos in New Zealand, with the first opened in 1994. This may be down to the fact that a lot of the winnings must go to public funds, making it less attractive for businesses to open multiple venues.

Current gambling legislation follows the Gambling Act of 2003, and is regulated by the Department of Internal affairs. With its strong emphasis on public funds and responsible gambling, New Zealand is an example of a country with a modern social outlook on land-based betting.

Internet Gambling in New Zealand

Online gambling is a little more complicated in New Zealand. The 2003 Gambling Act forbids domestic operators from running online casinos, to the point that only online lotteries and sports books affiliated with the country's authorities are allowed.

This might sound a tad extreme, until you see that the Act doesn't forbid foreign operators from offering games to New Zealand residents. This means that anyone of legal gambling age can set up an account with an overseas site. New Zealand players, therefore, will have to trust the gambling regulations of other countries, but they will find that many big casino sites accept their currency and allow them to play to their heart's content.

The reasoning behind banning domestic operators is powered by one thing: it's much more difficult to regulate online companies, and where their profit goes. For this reason, the Act prefers to keep things simple and issue an outright ban.

Note: For those unlicensed operators caught breaking the law, the punishment can be severe – with heavy fines and prison sentences the most likely outcomes.

Of course, things could always change, and the government could decide to allow online gambling. Laws are subject to amendments and may be altered from day to day, so it's always a good idea to do your own research before you start playing.
