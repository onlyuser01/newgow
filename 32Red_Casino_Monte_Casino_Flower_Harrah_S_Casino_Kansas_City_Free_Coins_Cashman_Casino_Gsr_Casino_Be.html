Land-based gambling in Canada

Since its formation at the end of the 19th century, the Canadian Criminal Code has permitted gambling under certain conditions, although this was quite restrictive: citizens could play some games of chance for charitable or religious purposes, but little else.

This changed in 1970, however, when drastic changes to the legislation allowed provinces to license and regulate gambling for the first time. The first commercial casinos appeared in Winnipeg and Montréal, followed by other provinces soon after, paving the way to the multi-billion-dollar gambling industry that exists today.

Canadian gambling laws are generally quite lenient, especially compared to the country's southern neighbor, the United States. Citizens can bet on casino games, sporting events and lottery-based games, including bingo and raffles.

Note: Depending on which of the country's ten provinces they live in, Canadian citizens may see varying forms of legislation. The Gaming Control Act of Ontario (1992), for example, aims to control the growth of gambling industry by targeting certain betting practises, such as lottery games. Citizens here may find gambling more restrictive than in other provinces.

Today, Canada seems to have struck a good balance between allowing land-based gambling to be a fun pastime, but with reasonable laws aimed at controlling problem betting. It's fair to say that Canada sets a good example for other nations to follow.

The Canadian gambling industry is regulated by a number of authorities whose job it is to check that gambling entities keep to the rules and provide the consumer with a safe playing experience.

Internet gambling in Canada

Canada has no specific law prohibiting online gambling, unlike many other countries. In fact, the topic is something of a grey area in Canadian law in that there is nothing that legalizes or prohibits the practice.

As such, it's perfectly safe and legal to play on a Canadian online casino as nothing forbids the user from doing so. Canadian law also doesn't stop players from using offshore casino sites, unlike in the USA.

One thing that is clear, however, is that, much like their bricks-and-mortar counterparts, unlicensed online casinos are strictly forbidden in Canada. Section 201(1) of the Canadian Code prohibits the 'keeping of a a common gaming or betting house': that is, an unlicensed establishment. There are substantial fines and even jail terms for operators who break this law.
