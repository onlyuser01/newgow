Land-based gambling in Brazil

Brazil's gambling culture has a rocky history. Authorized gambling was legal up until 1941 when the Social Democratic Party moved to 'preserve social morality' and outlawed most kinds of betting activity. The main reason behind this was gambling's links to organized crime, and the government wanted to introduce tough measures to combat it. Only two exceptions escaped the ban – lotteries and horse racing.

Much of this strict legislation is still in place today, although there have been several attempts to overturn it. Some activities, such as poker and sports betting, have seen the rules around them relaxed — Law No. 13,756/2018 introduced in 2019 effectively legalized sports betting — and it's possible for entities to apply for a license to offer them to clients.

Note: To understand which games are allowed in Brazil, it's useful to look at them through the prism of 'games of chance'. The more a game is seen to be down to chance, or luck, the less chance it has of it being permitted – this explains why poker, with all the skill involved, is generally accepted, while slots machines and roulette have had a tough time.

Recent years have seen a concerted effort from certain sectors to relax the restrictions on gambling further. Several important bills have been put before Brazilian Congress proposing the regulation of casinos and casino games, however these are subject to a long approval process and, as of December 2020, still haven't been passed.

Because of the strict legislation, there are only a handful of casinos in Brazil today and these only offer authorized betting games. There's a chance that more will appear in the future with possible reform on the horizon, but, until then, Brazil remains one of the most restrictive countries on the South American continent when it comes to gambling.

Internet gambling in Brazil

Brazilian law does not formally recognize online gambling as an activity, so the practice is generally treated similar to its land-based equivalent from a legal perspective.

Yet despite moves towards relaxing laws in the land-based industry, the government has maintained a consistent stance against online activity. In 2008 and 2010, legislative acts banning players from internet betting were brought before Congress but were ultimately voted down.

So, what does this all mean? Well, strict opposition means no domestic betting website has ever been created, however, there is no law against players in Brazil using foreign betting sites or online casinos. Many Portugal-based sites have taken advantage of their shared language by offering their services to a much bigger audience than in their own country.

That said, anti-virtual gambling movements still exist in Brazil and, if they are successful, then the law will change. For this reason, it's sensible to do your own research before gambling if you're in the country.
