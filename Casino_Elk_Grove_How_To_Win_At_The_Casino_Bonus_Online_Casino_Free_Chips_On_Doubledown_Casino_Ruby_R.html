A brief history of the Kahnawake license

The Kahnawake license dates back to a time when online gambling was little more than just an idea. In 1996, there were very few interactive casinos to speak of, and the practice of betting on the internet certainly wasn't widespread.

The Mohawk Council within the state, though, had the foresight to set up a special gaming commission to govern the forthcoming wave of internet betting.

A few years later, in 1999, it released its first set of regulations which ensured that all interactive gaming entities followed certain safety principles, including:

Ensuring fair, safe, and honest gaming at all times

Keeping gaming away from criminal activity

Paying winners promptly and fairly

Preventing minors and other vulnerable people from playing.

The Canadian legal system has never sought to challenge the legality of the Kahnawake Gaming law, recognizing it as a valid entity by default. In fact, when an unsuccessful license applicant sued the KGC in 2007, the Quebec Superior Court ruled in the KGC's favor.

The commission faced probably its biggest-ever issue in 2009 when it dealt with the Ultimate Bet cheating scandal. Quickly explained, the commission found multiple instances of illicit play on the Ultimate Bet poker site and fined owners Tokwiro Enterprises a total of $23.5 million dollars. Almost all of this went back to players who were cheated out of their money.

Today, the Kahnawake license is still a recognized certificate for online casinos to have, which is why you may come across sites that display its badge on their homepage. The license indicates a commitment to safety and fairness, and players can register a complaint about a member casino via its website.
