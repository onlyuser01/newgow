No deposit bonus legislation in the UK

Gambling has been a popular pastime in the United Kingdom for centuries, with betting on sports such as horse racing playing a significant role in British culture. Indeed, the Queen Elizabeth II herself is known to enjoy the odd 'flutter', as British people like to say, at Royal Ascot, the country's most famous annual racing event.

Casinos have also been around for several decades and are dotted around the country, so it was no surprise when online gambling started to become popular at the turn of the 21st century. The rising number of sports betting sites and UK online casinos since then has led to some very competitive promotions designed to hook the interest of online gamblers – one of them being the no deposit bonus.

Popular with UK gamblers thanks to its offer of free credit or spins without a need to place a deposit, the no deposit bonus has enjoyed widespread success in recent years and proved to be a useful marketing tool for UK casinos. They can also be a perk for the customer, giving them the chance to 'test drive' a new casino and possibly make a bit of money, albeit a modest amount which is subject to certain terms.

However, proposed law changes could change the face of the UK gambling industry, and the availability of no deposit bonuses as a result. In 2020, the government announced it was considering legislation that would clamp down on promotions that might encourage problem gambling, with bonuses deemed to be one of those. Under the proposals, gambling firms would have to comply with stricter advertising standards, including reducing gambling perks such as 'free bets', and also implement maximum stakes of £2 on online casino games. The changes would be enforced by the UK's Gambling Commission (GC), the government's gambling regulator.

Note: Although these reforms haven't been passed at the time of writing in April 2021, they have caused some operators to rethink their advertising strategy, with many already promoting the GC's 'When the Fun Stops, Stop' gambling awareness campaign, a necessary cause after the rapid increase in online betting during the Covid-19 pandemic.

Despite the measures, amateur UK gamblers don't have to pay tax on any no deposit bonus and associated winnings. That's because these casino earnings are tax-exempt under British law, although professional gamblers should declare it as part of their annual income.

We should point out, however, that it's a good idea to do your own research to support this information. Tax laws are liable to change at any time.

No deposit bonus types

No deposit bonuses come in two styles, both of which UK players can make use of: free credit or free spins.

The free credit option normally consists of a small figure that's added to your account right after you sign up. You might only be able to use this in certain games, and you can only access the winnings by meeting Wagering Requirements (WR). The WR is calculated by multiplying the bonus or winnings amount by a certain number of times, so if you get a $5 bonus with a x20 WR, you'll have to stake $100 to release your money.

Free spins are subject to similar terms, except your WR will have to be based on the winnings you earn from the initial spins. You might get as many as 50 free spins to use on certain slots games.
