Dash – a cryptocurrency with mainstream ambitions

Dash's story begins in January 2014, when Evan Duffield launched it as an open-source cryptocurrency called Xcoin.

Duffield created it as a fork of the Bitcoin protocol, meaning he took BTC's source code and converted it into a new, independent product. Like Bitcoin, it was decentralized, but attempted to improve on speed and privacy.

Much of its early growth was on the 'dark web' and the currency was even called Darkcoin for a period. However, negative press around this hidden part of the internet led to the coin's team giving it a full rebranding in March 2015, settling on the name Dash, a mix of 'digital' and 'cash'.

Dash's unit value stayed below $10 until the start of 2017 when it started to increase dramatically. By the end of the year, it had reached a high of $1,540, hitting a market cap of $4 billion and putting it in the top 12 cryptocurrencies in the world.

Like with any other crypto, however, Dash's value has been subject to market volatility. Its value in mid-2021 was a tad more than a tenth of the 2017 high, but it had improved in other areas in order to be considered as a mainstream payment method.

Dash gave its website a complete overhaul, created an app on both iOS and Android platforms and expanded its product range. In short, it became super user friendly as the Dash team looked to take on digital giants like PayPal.

Pros and cons of Dash

Dash prides itself on three key attributes: speed, security and privacy. It does this by using three system features called Masternodes, InstantSend and PrivateSend.

Masternodes are users that act as a kind of security agency via a special server. They help to ensure that transactions are both instant and private, and receive a share of newly mined Dash as a payment for their time and effort. Masternodes must prove their worth to the system by first paying a registration fee and then putting in hard work to help the system.

PrivateSend boosts Dash's privacy by concealing the user's details. Unlike the Bitcoin blockchain, which is completely transparent, Dash users can opt out of sharing the value of the transaction and the identity of each party's blockchain. This is a big advantage for many people, who prefer not to make their details public.

Cryptocurrencies have also been criticised for occasional transfer delays, which you typically wouldn't get with more traditional payment methods like Visa. For a small fee, Dash users can opt for InstantSend, where Masternodes verify a transaction in a few seconds as opposed to several minutes.

While we can view these features in a positive light, they do also conjure up a few questions. If Masternodes are a type of authority, can we really consider Dash to be truly decentralized? If PrivateSend conceals user identities, can we really rule out the possibility of illegal transactions? If we have to pay to send a payment instantly, is it better than many online platforms that offer this for free?

Note: These are all questions to consider when deciding to use Dash's services, but the bottom line is that the platform has a solid reputation for quick, safe payments and that you should feel secure when using it.
